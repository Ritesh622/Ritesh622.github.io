Research

My research focuses on communication-efficient learning, distributed inference, and signal processing, with applications spanning federated systems, wireless communication, and imaging pipelines.

Camera and Image Quality Engineering

As part of my work at Qualcomm, I focus on the evaluation and optimization of camera systems, with particular emphasis on image and video quality. This involves analyzing complex camera pipelines and improving their performance across diverse real-world scenarios. My work includes designing and developing tools for image quality assessment using MATLAB and Python, enabling systematic evaluation and efficient debugging of camera systems.

The primary goal of this work is to bridge the gap between algorithmic design and real-world deployment, ensuring that imaging systems perform robustly under varying conditions. This includes optimizing color reproduction, noise characteristics, and overall perceptual quality, while maintaining computational efficiency. Through this work, I gain practical insights into large-scale system design and real-time performance constraints.

Distributed Inference and Learning under Communication Constraints

My primary research during my Ph.D. focuses on developing communication-efficient algorithms for distributed systems, particularly in the context of federated learning. In modern large-scale networks, communication often becomes a bottleneck, and my work aims to understand and address the trade-off between estimation accuracy and communication cost in such systems.

I study distributed statistical inference problems where multiple clients collaboratively compute global quantities under strict communication constraints. This includes designing quantization schemes and compression techniques that reduce communication overhead while preserving statistical efficiency. A key aspect of this research is analyzing both theoretical limits and practical algorithms that operate effectively in bandwidth-limited environments.

In addition, I explore type-based quantization methods for discrete probability mass functions, which provide structured and efficient representations for distributed estimation tasks. My work combines tools from information theory, optimization, and machine learning to develop scalable solutions for real-world distributed systems.

RIS-Assisted NOMA/OMA Communication Systems

I have also worked on wireless communication systems involving Reconfigurable Intelligent Surfaces (RIS) and hybrid NOMA/OMA schemes. This work focuses on improving spectral efficiency and system performance by leveraging intelligent signal reflection and advanced multiple access techniques.

In particular, I studied the impact of practical imperfections such as imperfect successive interference cancellation (SIC) and phase compensation errors in RIS-assisted systems. The goal is to design robust communication strategies that maintain performance under non-ideal conditions. This line of work integrates concepts from signal processing, wireless communication theory, and system optimization.
