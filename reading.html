Further Reading

This is a small collection of resources that I have been using while learning and exploring. It is not meant to be complete, but rather a running list of material that I find useful over time. I am deeply grateful to my advisor, Prof. Shashank Vatedka, for his constant guidance and support. Much of what I have learned, and many of the perspectives reflected here, are shaped by his mentorship.

Learning Resources

I often begin with Coursera when I am unfamiliar with a topic, as the structured format helps in building a foundation.

MIT OpenCourseWare is useful when I want more detailed and rigorous lectures, even though it can be quite dense at times.

I have used NPTEL extensively during my coursework, and it aligns well with standard academic curricula.

I came across the Flower documentation while exploring federated learning. It provides a practical perspective that complements theoretical understanding.

I occasionally watch talks from the Simons Institute, which offer exposure to advanced topics and current research directions.

Books and Notes

Project Gutenberg is a useful source for non-technical reading.

Archive.org has been helpful for accessing older or otherwise hard-to-find material.

Open Library is convenient when I want to quickly look up a book.

I have occasionally referred to notes from the AMS, although I am still becoming comfortable with them.

Tools I Use

ILovePDF is something I use frequently to clean and compress PDFs.

I use Overleaf for writing and collaborating on documents in LaTeX.

For managing references, I find Zotero quite useful.

I frequently use arXiv to follow recent developments and explore new ideas.

Google Scholar is helpful for finding related work and tracking citations.

For quick experiments and prototyping, I use Google Colab.

Reading Papers

This guide on how to read a paper helped me understand how to approach research papers more effectively.

I also found this Stanford guide useful when I initially felt uncertain about how to begin reading research literature.

Writing

The Chicago Manual of Style is a helpful reference for improving clarity in writing.

I have referred to “How to Write Mathematics”: link, which, although dense, provides valuable insights.

I often read Paul Graham’s essays for their clarity and simplicity.

Further Reading

Terence Tao’s blog — I do not fully understand everything yet, but it is inspiring and offers insight into how deep mathematical thinking develops.

Cal Newport — provides useful perspectives on focus and structured learning.

LessWrong — contains discussions on reasoning and clear thinking, which I explore occasionally.

Paul Graham essays — I revisit these frequently for their clarity and simplicity.

Distill — provides intuitive and well-structured explanations of complex ideas.

Quanta Magazine — presents advanced topics in science and mathematics in an accessible way.
